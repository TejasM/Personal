What I did at SharpScholar

My official title at SharpScholar was CTO. Like many startups this is the default role that inexperienced technical co-founders take on. Now I know that this was the wrong job title, though that didn't really impact the work I did. At a high level, I took on two key roles: Product Manager and Full Stack Developer. Remember, all this is in hindsight. When I first decided to this, my approach was far more simplistic and surface level. I saw a team with a sense of an idea that need a technical lead to execute.

For the majority of the first three months, I just worked on developing the software. Using my reasonably honed skill to get shit done, I was able to hack together what was essentially a minimum viable product. My team was then able to push this product out to various universities and professors and thus begin the cycle of learning about product management, development, marketing, and business. While a lot of the business lessons learnt at SharpScholar came as result of being the education field, a lot of the product side learns happened in a broader sense.

As time went by and the product became more stable, time came for me to grow beyond a full stack developer. I now was in a position to give the product a sense of direction. And there were many possibilities. From analytics, to mashware, to personalized learning, to peer learning we had many possible directions we could have gone in. As such my role in the company evolved into one that I was quite unfamiliar with but one I relished.

At this stage of the company, I was able to better articulate what my responsibilities were:

  • Set the vision for the product as the company evolves. Translate the abstract vision of “Empowering scholars around the world” to meaningful and understandable short term and long term visions. Champion where the product will go as a result.

  • Translate user market research, sales reports, and user behaviour into tangible set of specifications of product features, goals and visions.

    • Top Level: Long Term and Short Term Product Vision

    • Middle Level: Monthly product roadmap and associated set of projects

    • Bottom Level: Weekly updates to the team

  • Software Development

  • Quality Assurance

  • Security

  • Data Analytics on User Behaviour

Of course my focus depended highly on what the startup needed at the time. If the product was coming under fire due to technical issues, I would put on my Software developer/QA hat and get to coding. If there was some breathing space, then I would start looking to build process and systems that would allow me to understand users better, set product roadmaps, and plan/execute for the future.

In theory this approach works well but it suffered in multiple ways. First, it was an unfocused and reactive approach. No software is bug free, especially one built by one developer with minimal experience. I was constantly reacting to fires rather than building the company I wanted to be building. As soon as I gained some momentum in setting direction, I would be called away to solve existing issues. As such this result in considerable mental fatigue, leading to the second issue. Since I was acting as both the product manager and developer, I was often unable to keep the two separate. For instance, instead of fully specing a feature and understanding the story behind it I would jump into coding and try to get it done. Even more so, I should have been able to see the technical debt that was being built up as a result. Failing to balance the two roles and viewpoints, caused me to perform both of them poorly.

As I write this series of posts to breakdown SharpScholar, I aim to build up insights on how to potentially do it better. I have recently read Systems and processes to build products people love and look forward to using my experience to create a process I will take moving forward.
